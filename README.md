# Nigeria Fuel Subsidy Removal: Blessing or Curse?
Three years ago, Nigeria abolished a fuel subsidy that had existed for nearly five decades. The fiscal implication was straightforward: the country was spending N4.4 trillion per year to keep pump prices at N184 per liter while import parity had already reached N590. The ceiling was not cheap. It was expensive, regressive, and economically wasteful.

What followed the reform is evidence that encapsulates both the protagonist's and the antagonist's points of view, which the empirical studies have now confirmed. Against this backdrop, my working paper applies eight microeconomic frameworks to Nigeria's petroleum market across 34 months from May 2023 through March 2026, using institutional data from NMDPRA, the IMF, the World Bank, NBS, and CBN.

Key findings: N750 billion annual deadweight loss from the price ceiling; 76.2 percent consumer incidence of the reform price shock; Cournot duopoly supply ratio prediction corroborated by NMDPRA data to within 3.6 percent; a Lerner Index of 0.42 at the October 2024 monopoly peak; N23 to N52 per liter uncorrected Pigouvian externality; Cobb-Douglas increasing returns to scale of 1.07 at the Dangote refinery; and a social protection gap of N3.87 trillion between fiscal savings and deployed cash transfers. 

The reform achieved its fiscal objectives, but the social protection component did not. The gap between N4.4 trillion in annual savings and N528 billion in cash transfers actually deployed is not a technical problem. It is a political economy decision and structural bottleneck that prevents the reform from reaching the common citizen.
